Jobseeker Support — Policy and Eligibility Overview (Sample Reference Document)

PURPOSE
This document is a self-contained, synthetic sample modelled on New Zealand
Ministry of Social Development (MSD) welfare guidance. It exists so the DocSense
evaluation harness has something concrete to ingest and ask questions about when
the Qdrant vector store is otherwise empty. The figures below are illustrative
and must not be relied on for real entitlement decisions.

1. WHAT JOBSEEKER SUPPORT IS
Jobseeker Support is a weekly income-support payment for people who are not in
full-time employment and who are either actively looking for work, or temporarily
unable to work because of a health condition, injury, or disability. It replaced
the former Unemployment Benefit and Sickness Benefit, bringing both groups under
a single payment with a shared set of obligations.

2. CORE ELIGIBILITY CRITERIA
To qualify for Jobseeker Support a person must meet all of the following:
- Be aged 18 years or older. People aged 19 with dependent children may also
  qualify under the same category.
- Be a New Zealand citizen or hold permanent residence, and ordinarily live in
  New Zealand.
- Have lived in New Zealand for a continuous period of at least two years at any
  one time since becoming a citizen or resident.
- Not be in full-time employment, defined as 30 or more hours of paid work per
  week.
- Have income and cash assets below the relevant thresholds described in
  sections 4 and 5.

3. WORK OBLIGATIONS
Most people receiving Jobseeker Support have work obligations. This means they
must take reasonable steps to find and prepare for work, including:
- Attending appointments and seminars arranged by their case manager.
- Preparing or updating a curriculum vitae.
- Applying for suitable job vacancies and accepting offers of suitable work.
A person whose Jobseeker Support is granted on the ground of a health condition
may have their work obligations deferred while that condition is assessed. Failure
to meet obligations without a good and sufficient reason can result in a sanction
that reduces or suspends the payment.

4. INCOME THRESHOLDS AND ABATEMENT
Jobseeker Support is income-tested. A single recipient can earn up to 160 dollars
gross per week before their payment begins to reduce. For every dollar of income
above that threshold, the weekly payment reduces — abates — by 70 cents. Once
income reaches the point where the payment would fall to zero, entitlement ends
for that week. Partnered recipients have the combined household income assessed
against a higher joint threshold.

5. ASSET TEST
Unlike the main benefit payment, Jobseeker Support itself is not directly asset
tested for the base entitlement; however, cash assets are relevant to supplementary
assistance. For supplementary help such as an Advance Payment of Benefit, a single
person is generally expected to hold no more than 1,257 dollars in immediately
available cash assets, and a couple no more than 2,094 dollars, before that extra
help is reduced. The family home and an ordinary motor vehicle are not counted as
cash assets.

6. STAND-DOWN PERIOD
A new grant of Jobseeker Support usually carries a stand-down period before the
first payment is made. The standard stand-down is one to two weeks, and the exact
length depends on the income the person or their partner earned in the 26 weeks
before applying. People who were made redundant or who earned higher incomes
before applying generally face a longer stand-down than those who had little or no
recent income.

7. 26-WEEK REAPPLICATION
A recent change to welfare settings requires some Jobseeker Support clients to
reapply for their payment every 26 weeks, rather than every 52 weeks as was
previously the case. The shorter reapplication cycle is intended to bring clients
into more regular contact with their case manager, refresh their work plan, and
confirm that their circumstances and obligations are still current. Clients are
notified ahead of their reapplication date and can complete the process online.

8. PAYMENT RATES
Net weekly rates depend on a person's situation. As an illustrative example, a
single person aged 25 or over with no children might receive about 337 dollars net
per week, while a single person aged 20 to 24 with no children might receive a
lower rate of about 278 dollars net per week. Sole parents are paid under a
different category, Sole Parent Support, rather than Jobseeker Support.

9. SUPPLEMENTARY ASSISTANCE
People on Jobseeker Support may also qualify for additional, separately assessed
help, including the Accommodation Supplement toward rent, board, or mortgage
costs; a Disability Allowance for ongoing additional costs caused by a health
condition; and Temporary Additional Support as a last resort for essential living
costs that cannot be met from income. Each of these has its own eligibility rules
and is not automatic.

10. RELATIONSHIP TO EMPLOYMENT SUPPORT
The policy intent behind Jobseeker Support is to provide income while actively
moving people into sustainable employment. Case managers can refer clients to
employment programmes, training, work-confidence seminars, and industry
partnerships. Moving into work of 30 or more hours per week ends the Jobseeker
Support entitlement, but recipients may still qualify for in-work supplementary
assistance depending on their income and family circumstances.
